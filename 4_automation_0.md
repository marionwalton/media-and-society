# GenAI & automated media production
{: .no_toc }

*By Marion Walton, University of Cape Town*

If you’ve used a chatbot such as ChatGPT (OpenAI), Meta AI, Copilot (Microsoft), Claude (Anthropic), if you’ve used Gemini (Google) for search, or Canva AI, DaVinci AI, Dall-E or Midjourney to generate synthetic images and video you’ve interacted with **[Generative Artificial Intelligence](definitions.html#generative-ai) (GenAI) models**. 

GenAI usually takes the form of a chat interface to a statistical model, which returns responses (e.g. text, images, videos) to user **prompts**. These interactions are a type of automated media production currently being adopted in tech and media industries as well as more broadly. For example, In 2026, [Netflix reported](https://www.theverge.com/streaming/966633/netflix-ai-titles-q2-2026-earnings) that 300 of their titles had used GenAI in production.

This chapter provides an introductory explanation of how the models and systems underlying **Generative AI** 
 are used to create synthetic media and briefly addresses questions about using GenAI in your academic work, and the potential impact of GenAI on work in media industries.

### What is GenerativeAI?

The term "[Artificial Intelligence](definitions.html#artificial-intelligence)" (AI) refers to a wide range of technologies, industries and areas of research. The term is used very loosely, often for marketing purposes ([Bender, 2026](definitions.html#bender_2026)). Generative AI (GenAI) technologies are highly relevant to you as a student in Media Studies.  For now we'll focus on defining GenAI and explaining how these systems work. 

{: .definition}
Generative Artificial Intelligence or _[Generative AI](definitions.html#generative-AI)_ systems are statistical techniques used to automate media production by computers. The techniques work by summarising and then synthesizing similar patterns to those found in the media copied as a “training” dataset from sources such as the wikipedia, company websites and social media posts. The training data is encoded in generative models used to produce synthetic text, images, and other forms of media in response to a user's prompt. GenAI systems include well known commercial systems such as ChatGPT (OpenAI), Gemini (Google), Claude (Anthropic) or Copilot (Microsoft). 

### What is a GenAI model?
A GenAI model (e.g. LLM or GANS) models a training dataset. When we use the word "model" in this way, we mean a **statistical model**, as used in machine learning, which is not an exact replica or copy of the training dataset. Instead, it's intended to be used to synthesize data from the training dataset to generate similar texts and images to those included in the training data. 

{: .definition}
A _generative model_ is a machine learning model designed to synthesize new data which is similar to its training data. Generative models rely on training data which can include text, images and other forms of media and produce synthetic media. There are several types of generative model, such as Large Language Models (LLMs), which encode text, or diffusion models, which encode images. 

### What is a prompt?
{: .definition}
Generative AI systems respond to _prompts_ from users as input. Prompts can include text and various forms of digital media.

In future courses, we'll study techniques for prompting. The more you know about a subject, the more you'll command the specialised language needed to control the output of a GenAI system. This knowledge also makes it easier to evaluate GenAI output using your own knowledge of the topic.

### How do generative models work?

#### Large language models
The generative models in chatbots which produce synthetic text are known as Large Language Models (LLMs). 

{: .definition}
Large Language Models include OpenAI’s ChatGPT, Google’s Gemini, Anthropic's Claude and Meta’s Llama. LLMs are trained on massive collections of text, are able to generate lengthy plausible sounding text answers and simulate convincing conversations fluently. 

LLMs work by predicting the most likely words to follow your prompt, based on patterns recorded in a statistical model created from the words in billions of human-created sources (such as online news articles, Wikipedia, social media posts etc). 
 
#### Visual and multimodal models
While language models predict the most likely next word, visual models predict the most likely pixel based on patterns they've encoded from downloading and processing many online photographs and artworks (many without payment or permission from the creators). This visual data is matched with the prompts that users enter. 

{: .definition}
_Multimodal_ means that a model processes text, image, and audio. Multimodal prompting combines LLMs and visual models. For example, text-to-image GenAI systems encode verbal user prompts and match them with statistical models created from the labels of the images in the training data. It then uses the encoded user prompt to identify matching patterns of pixels in the training data which are used to synthesize an image. 

As we've already learned, this output is known as synthetic media. Once you understand how GenAI works, you should find it easier to evaluate whether and when to use a text, image or video which is synthesized with GenAI.

## Concerns about GenAI

Our focus in this course is the potential representational harms which come about as a result of reliance on training data which reflects the gendered, racist, classist and other discourses and media images around us.

In future courses, we will discuss the ethical concerns which have led many people to call for tighter control of GenAI industries or to refuse GenAI completely.

### Environmental costs

While we tend to picture GenAI as cute little chatbots, they have a substantial environmental footprint. The massive data centres used to house, power and cool these systems has led the tech giants to abandon their "net zero"goals in the past few years. I[n Cape Town, planned data centres](https://www.greenbuildingafrica.co.za/cape-town-approves-large-scale-data-centre-development-despite-concerns-over-water-and-energy-use/) are expected to put serious pressure on land, water and electricity, all of which are  already in short supply.

### Whose language?
Despite the fact that so many sources have been used to train LLMs, the training data and consequently the linguistic patterns LLMs reproduce are dominated by English and other "high resource" languages (languages which are well represented online).  African languages, although they comprise 30% of spoken languages, are far less well represented online or in digital resources used by LLMs ([Matsilele & Tshuma , 2025](references.html#matsilele_2025)). Using chatbots means we're missing out on the practices and perspectives of many African language speakers. 

### Protecting privacy
When prompting a model, any information you provide (including highly personal information) is being harvested by the system to create a model of you. 

Free systems are using all the information your provide to train their own model and consequently may repeat information from your prompts in output to other users or hackers.  In addition, many people accidentally publish their interactions, leaving their chats exposed and making them vulnerable to hackers, identity theft, stalkers and other harms. 

You should view GenAI use as a major privacy risk and think carefully about anything you disclose in your chats. You should also not upload copyrighted material such as published articles as part of your prompt. 

## GenerativeAI and media industries 

Used as media production tools, the use of generative models to generate synthetic media is leading to dramatic shifts in both professional and everyday media practices. The rapid uptake of these systems is having major consequences for the media production, software and natural ecosystems. 

### Environment
The so-called "hyperscaler" companies, namely Alphabet (Google), Amazon (AWS), Microsoft, OpenAI and Meta (Facebook) have invested billions of dollars to build the immense data center networks needed for GenerativeAI infrastructure. These systems consume huge amounts of electricity while generating so much heat that they require water-intensive cooling systems, leading to many community protests about their environmental impact. 

### Industry and society
While AI companies rely heavily on media as (unpaid) training data, chatbot users are less likely to turn to traditional sources of information, thus lowering readership of news organisations. As a result, companies like Alphabet and OpenAI have disproportionate power as highly centralised gatekeepers controlling access to information.

Governments are struggling to respond with new laws in time to curb the social, cultural and environmental impact of GenAI. In the meantime, rapid adoption of GenAi is also changing the nature of work and employment in media industries. With cheaper content production automated, entry-level routes to employment in the media industry are under threat while media owners pursue lower costs and greater efficiency and marketers and advertisers seek more precise targeting for potential customers. 

## Activity - How it works

### Training data
{: .definition}
The text used to build a language model is known as the model's **training data** since it provides the source material which the model will use to synthesise and output text. 

Such training data is copied from online sources such as Wikipedia, and (usually without permission) from digital versions of books and websites.  

### From text to numbers

The text from the training data is broken up into tokens (in simple terms, a token is a word or a part of a word), which is converted to numbers (known as an embedding). 

When the user generates a text prompt, the prompt is also tokenised and converted to numbers. These numbers are then matched with numbers from the training data.

### Finding the most probable next word

During a process known as pre-training, a transformer powers the model to answer the following question: 

	"What is the most probable next word/token that will follow this input?". 

Statistical **probabilities** are used to work out which word/token should come next in the output text. 

Transformers capture **contextual information** i.e. the relationships between the words/tokens in the prompt (the co-text). This technique helps to ensure that synonyms are not confused e.g. [river] bank vs. bank [account]. 

Finally the numbers are converted back to words and the model outputs a text signifier (response to the user's prompt).

LLMs are good at copying the textual patterns in writing. Their output requires verification, and they should not be used where accuracy is important.

## Multimodal LLMs
{: .definition}
Multimodal LLMs can generate text as well as images and other forms of media. 

### Training data for multimodal LLMs

GANS and Diffusion image generation models collect huge datasets of text-image pairs (e.g. images and their descriptions copied from the web) as their training data. Various models are trained on different types of image data e.g. Midjourney is notorious for having been trained on artworks without permission.

### Image -> numbers <- text description
These types of models are trained to convert an **image and its text description** into similar numbers. 

### Prompt -> numbers -> image
When prompted with a text prompt, the model converts the text prompt into numbers (much as an LLM would), and then generates visual signifiers which are a numeric match for the prompt.

Synthetic images are superficially cohesive because GenAI models reconstruct their training data. Generating logical coherence in images is challenging (e.g. differentiating between a left and a right arm).
